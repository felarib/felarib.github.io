My research agenda sits at the intersection of organized crime governance, state capacity, and political violence in Latin America, with Brazil as my primary empirical site. Methodologically, I rely on panel data, spatial econometrics, and causal inference to identify how criminal organizations govern territory and how state action — particularly infrastructure investment and security policy — shapes patterns of violence.

Current projects
======

**The logistical violence premium.** My dissertation (Temple University, 2025) used a staggered difference-in-differences design across 184 municipalities in Ceará, Brazil, combined with eleven months of fieldwork including the Port of Pecém, to show that infrastructure construction under Brazil's Growth Acceleration Program (PAC) increased homicides. A paper building on this finding, "Paving the Way for Violence," is currently under revise-and-resubmit at *Latin American Politics and Society* (see [Publications](/publications/)).

**Kingpin decapitation and criminal governance.** An ongoing project building a dataset and estimation pipeline comparing the governance structures of the PCC (São Paulo) and the Comando Vermelho (Rio de Janeiro), examining how the removal of criminal leadership affects post-removal violence outcomes. The pipeline draws on Fogo Cruzado and ISP-Rio data.

**Book project: *State Authority at the Infrastructure Frontier*.** A prospective book extending the dissertation's core finding — that state-led infrastructure development can generate, rather than resolve, public security problems — into a broader account of development and security as joint products of state action.

**Framing-stage project: organized crime in Lusophone Africa.** Early-stage work extending my approach to criminal governance and state capacity to Mozambique and other Lusophone African contexts.

Methods
======
Panel data, spatial econometrics, and causal inference — including modern staggered difference-in-differences estimators (Callaway–Sant'Anna, Sun–Abraham, LP-DiD).
