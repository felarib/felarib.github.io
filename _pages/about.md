I am a Postdoctoral Fellow at the Sam Nunn School of International Affairs, Georgia Institute of Technology. My research examines organized crime governance, state capacity, and political violence in Latin America, with Brazil as my primary empirical site. I use panel data, spatial econometrics, and causal inference to study how criminal organizations govern territory and how state infrastructure and security policy interact to produce (or prevent) violence.

I defended my Ph.D. in Political Science at Temple University in April 2025. My dissertation used a staggered difference-in-differences design across 184 municipalities in Ceará, Brazil — combined with eleven months of fieldwork, including at the Port of Pecém — to show that infrastructure construction under Brazil's Growth Acceleration Program (PAC) increased homicides through what I term the "logistical violence premium."

I currently teach Comparative Political Violence and Government and People in Latin America at Georgia Tech, and previously taught at Macalester College. I am fluent in Portuguese and Spanish, with French in progress.
